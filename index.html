üö© Red Flags to Watch For

  • 1. Urgent or Threatening Language
    Phrases like "Act now," "Verify immediately," or "Your account will be suspended" create false urgency to bypass your critical thinking. Legitimate organizations rarely create panic.
  • 2. Unexpected Credential Requests
    Real IT teams will NEVER ask you to enter your password via an email link. Any request to "verify," "confirm," or "update" credentials via email is suspicious.
  • 3. Suspicious Sender Address
    Always check the full email address, not just the display name. "IT Services" could be from attackers@fake-company.com. Hover over the sender to see the real address.
  • 4. Generic Greetings
    Phishing emails often use "Dear User," "Dear Customer," or just "Hello" instead of your name. Companies that know you typically personalize their communications.
  • 5. Suspicious Links
    Before clicking any link, hover your mouse over it to see the real URL. If it doesn't match the supposed sender's domain or looks suspicious (strange characters, unfamiliar extension like .xyz, .tk), don't click.
  • 6. Poor Grammar or Spelling
    Professional companies proofread their emails. Multiple typos, awkward phrasing, or grammatical errors are red flags.

‚úÖ What You Should Do

  • PAUSE Before Clicking
    Take a moment to evaluate unexpected emails, especially those requesting action. Urgency is a tactic‚Äîslow down and think critically.
  • HOVER Over Links
    Place your mouse over any link (don't click) to see where it really goes. Check if the URL matches the supposed sender's legitimate domain.
  • VERIFY Through Separate Channels
    If an email requests sensitive action, verify it independently. Call the sender using a known phone number (not one in the email), or navigate to their website directly by typing the URL.
  • REPORT Suspicious Emails
    Use the "Report Phishing" button in Outlook/Gmail, or forward to . Even if you're unsure, report it‚Äîfalse positives are better than missed attacks.
  • ENABLE Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)
    MFA provides critical protection. Even if attackers steal your password, they cannot access your account without the second factor (code from your phone, security key, etc.).
  • UPDATE Your Software
    Keep your operating system, browser, and applications up to date. Security patches fix vulnerabilities that attackers exploit.
